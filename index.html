Welcome to the Serengeti

The Serengeti National Park in Tanzania, Africa, is an amazing place filled with over 400 species of fauna.

The Serengeti is most famouns for its migration of millions of wildebeest. It is a breathtaking sight that never ends, a loop going clockwise around the Serengeti, even going into Kenya in the north. However, this is not all the Serengeti is famous for.

The Serengeti spans over 30,000 square kilometers (12,000 square miles). This is an area larger than the state of Connecticut. This is essentially split up into 3 sections. The southern/central part of the park, also known as Seronera Valley, is the most popular area of the Serengeti. The Maasai people who live in the area call it "serengit", meaning "the land of endless plains." Indeed, it is all plains and savannah. It has sparse vegetation, but is teeming with wildlife. This is where most of safaris would go.

The western area in the Serengeti is mostly defined by the Grumeti River. This area is more forest and dense vegetation, and so is harder to move around. Also, there is not too much wildlife (besides the bugs and small animals). The northernmost part, the Lobo area, meets up with the Masai Mara Reserve at the Kenyan border. This is the least visited area, as there is not much here.

Two World Heritage sites and 2 Biosphere Reserves were established within the Serengeti. This is because of it's unique ecosystem, which is actually one of the oldest on the earth - and surprisingly, hasn't changed much! The biodiversity is astonishing. There are the Big Five - lions, leopards, rhinos, cape buffalo, and elephants - and the Little Five - antlions, rhino beetles, elephant shrews, buffalo weavers, and leopard tortoises - that truly give the Serengeti its reputation. In fact, there are over 540 species of birds alone in the Serengeti, and many different kinds of plants.
